## 2025 Winners

### [AFRICA MINERAL PULSE: DESIGNING AGENCY IN AFRICA’S EXTRACTIVE ECONOMIES](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1lRcsayMPlKhhbxoIh7QCM62nqmaJUEr2/view?usp=drive_link)
**Fas Lebbie**

This research proposal builds upon my doctoral research in Transition Design to explore how mineral transitions in Africa can be actualized by increasing access to data, thereby enhancing choices for designing sustainable mineral futures. The pulse model proposes three categories: de-risking investment for sustainable mining practices, developing local beneficiation infrastructure, and redistributing mineral wealth to local communities. The design created an interactive map of Africa using six data indices—Extractive Dependence Index, Mineral Exploration Activity, Mineral Resource Contracts, Green Transition Minerals, Community Beneficiation, and Local Processing Capacity & Infrastructure—to transform how individuals and communities understand their contexts and make choices aligned with their values and intentions.

Drawing on Heinz von Foerster’s cybernetic principle of increasing choices, the design integrates five key cybernetic principles—increasing human agency, maintaining requisite variety, achieving dynamic stability, applying second-order cybernetics, and expanding meaningful choices—to transform Africa’s extractive systems from passive resource provision to active community stewardship. It aims to shift communities from being mineral resource providers to being system stewards, capable of defining and navigating their own mineral futures.

### [INTRANOTE: PROTOTYPING AN AI-DRIVEN DESIGN RATIONALE SYSTEM SUPPORTING PARTICIPATORY CONVERSATIONS](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1HDzoVrK9XYtmhexNx87IlF-rCXUcLjda/view?usp=drive_link)
**Franklin Xu, Yixi (Chris) Wu, Yuhan (Max) Wu, Minghao Xu**

We proposed IntraNote, an AI-assisted Design Rationale System (DRS) that supports participatory design in complex, multi-stakeholder settings. Focusing on conversations in design charrettes, the system captures both dialogue and spatial interactions, allowing participants to document and revisit discussions, pose targeted questions post-hoc, and trace the rationale behind design decisions. By making the structure of conversation visible, IntraNote helps participants reflect, identify shared values, and engage with the perspectives of others. Through the implementation of a technical demo and a pilot study (N = 18), we observed that question-driven interaction emerged naturally, prompting users to explore alternatives, recognize trade-offs, and articulate their positions more clearly.

Based on these results, we further conceptualize the system as a symbiotic interface that provides scaffolding information to trigger deeper questioning, allowing participants to explore shared trajectories from their own perspectives. The system contributes to increasing human agency by expanding the space of meaningful choices—not by offering more options, but by making them more intelligible, relevant, and grounded in shared inquiry. Overall, we discuss how IntraNote supports documentation, reflection, and collaborative problem-finding, offering a cybernetic approach to navigating wicked problems through question-centered interaction.

## 2025 Runners-Up

### THE RIF IMPACT FRAMEWORK: LANGUAGE, LEGIBILITY, AND DESIGN FOR GOVERNMENT CAPACITY
**Liv Schaefer, Amy Kang, Jaimie An, David Fuentes**

The RIF Impact Framework is a cybernetic intervention designed to address the growing invisibility of workforce disruptions across the U.S. federal government. Amid ongoing reductions in force (RIFs) driven by policy, attrition, and restructuring, the public sector lacks a coherent method to track and interpret what capabilities are being lost, undermining democratic oversight and future planning. Developed by graduate students at Carnegie Mellon University, this lightweight, ethics-driven framework combines a structured data schema (CSV/YAML) with a reflexive practice grounded in systems thinking.

It enables documentation of lost capacity by mapping job functions, program impacts, and geographic relevance, while embedding narrative context and data provenance. More than a technical solution, the framework invites institutions and stakeholders to engage in second-order observation: to reflect on their role within systems and to design with awareness. In doing so, it aims to shift civil service toward a more adaptive, transparent, and ethically responsive model of governance. Positioned at the intersection of public interest technology, labor theory, and cybernetics, the RIF Impact Framework offers both a language and a lens for stewarding the state’s capacity in an age of complexity, supporting collective foresight and institutional memory.

### [FISHERWOMEN OF MUMBAI: RETHINKING THE CYCLES OF WORK & WEALTH](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1oVqjVTH12LeV0CP6LJeTUJsm0Wh2_QIa/view?usp=drive_link)
**Sagarika Kulkarni**

Mumbai’s fisherwomen sustain their livelihoods through deeply rooted commoning practices, solidarity networks, self-help groups, shared reproductive labor, and informal financial pacts. These systems provide economic and social resilience, yet the women remain unrecognized within the city’s formal structures. As environmental degradation, overfishing, monsoon disruptions, and systemic gender biases threaten their stability, the lack of institutional support leaves them increasingly vulnerable.

This research explores how formalizing fisherwomen’s existing informal networks and practices through cooperative models can strengthen their economic security, ensuring the year-round functioning of fishing and allied value chains. By mapping their daily routines, this research identifies critical intervention points where cooperative frameworks can be incrementally introduced to enhance financial stability and care infrastructure. It also examines the spatial implications of these interventions, proposing design strategies that align with their needs. Through ethnographic research, urban analysis, and collaboration with potential stakeholders, this study envisions a cooperative-driven model that not only reinforces Mumbai’s fishing economy but also integrates spaces of care and community support.

## Context & Purpose

### Inaugural Year
In this inaugural year of the #NewMacy Cybernetics Prize, there are two winners tied for first place. Because of the outstanding quality of the two highest-rated submissions, the closeness of their rating, and their compelling cybernetic solutions covering two very different wicked challenges, the Judging Panel decided to split the Prize evenly between them. In May 2025 we received 30 submissions from a wide range of CMU departments and years of study. This total number was unexpected and speaks to CMU students’ widespread interest in wicked challenges and cybernetic/systemic techniques for approaching them. Two-thirds of the submissions were led by masters students, roughly one-quarter were PhDs, and roughly one-tenth were undergrads. Nearly two-thirds were solo submissions and the one-third remaining were teams of 2 to 4. Several submissions were from CMU Africa. Eleven submissions were from [School of Architecture](https://soa.cmu.edu), 10 School of Design, 4 Human-Computer Interaction Institute, 1 each from Humanities & Social Sciences (Dietrich), Engineering, Entertainment Technology Center, Public Policy (Heinz College), and Business (Tepper School). For a full description of the Prize, eligibility, criteria, and intentions, see the #NewMacy Cybernetics Prize Page.

### Purpose of the Prize
The #NewMacy Cybernetics Prize creates incentives for developing projects grounded in Cybernetics, especially by using that discipline’s rigor, formalisms, and models to invite novel interventions to wicked challenges in ecological, social, and/or technological contexts. The Prize competition inspires student-scholars to develop design proposals that expand human agency, possibility, and choice in a defined context. Empathetic incorporation of the value systems of participants through design conversations is required, from close-in as well as the broader collective community, thereby bringing in 21st-century approaches to design. A complete description of the Prize’s Intentions and Criteria for Judging are here. The Laboratory for Cybernetics (Lab4C) will maintain a repository of the four finalists that will be shared with an international community of student-scholars and in-world practitioners as one facet of a growing collective of organizations that will contribute to and benefit from the aggregate case studies and experiences of the whole group.

### Why "#NewMacy" in 2025 Prize Title
The Cybernetics Prize was first established in 2025 and was named after the #NewMacy Initiative, which revisited and revised a series of carefully curated conversations from 1945 through 1953, comprising scholars across a wide swath of the hard and soft sciences. These original Macy Conferences catalyzed the broad influence of Cybernetics on all other disciplines throughout the second half of the 20th century. #NewMacy as a modern hashtag was coined in 2020 in response to the global COVID pandemic and the recognition of many concurrent wicked challenges occurring at global scale. Building on the original Macy Conferences’ innovation with their trans-disciplinary emphasis, #NewMacy adds focus on the trans-geographical (spanning cultures and crossing boundaries of nation, governance, and history) and trans-generational (conversation across age cohorts) as foundational pillars for cybernetic collaborations.
